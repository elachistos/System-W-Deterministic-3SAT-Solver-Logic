# Is the following procedure over a restricted propositional language complete and computable in polynomial time?

The vocabulary of $W$ is specified as follows: a set of propositional variables, where each variable is a symbol which may be a number, a letter (uppercase or lowercase), a letter with a subscript, or any other atomic symbol; a set of connectives {$\land, \to$}; a set of parentheses {$(, )$}; and $\bot$.

For each variable $A$, the literals are $A^+$ and $A^-$. The complement operation on literals is defined by $(A^+)^c = A^-$ and $(A^-)^c = A^+$. One may optionally write $A^+$ as $A$ and $A^-$ as $\neg A$.

### Grammar:

1. $\bot$ is a sentence of $W$.
2. If $L_1$ and $L_2$ are literals, then $(L_1 \land L_2)$ is a sentence of $W$.
3. If $L_1, L_2, L_3, L_4$ are literals, then $(L_1 \land L_2) \to (L_3 \land L_4)$ is a sentence of $W$.
4. Nothing else is a sentence of $W$.

### Semantics:

An interpretation $\mathscr{I}$ of $W$ is an assignment to each literal exactly one truth value in {$\text{True}, \text{False}$}, such that for any literal $L$, $\mathscr{I}(L) = \text{True}$ iff $\mathscr{I}(L^c) = \text{False}$; and an assignment to each sentence of $W$ exactly one truth value in {$\text{True}, \text{False}$}, as set out below.

$\mathscr{I}(\bot)$ is always False.

$\mathscr{I}((L_1 \land L_2)) = \text{True}$ iff $\mathscr{I}(L_1) = \text{True}$ and $\mathscr{I}(L_2) = \text{True}$.

$\mathscr{I}((L_1 \land L_2) \to (L_3 \land L_4)) = \text{True}$ iff $\mathscr{I}(L_3) = \text{True}$ and $\mathscr{I}(L_4) = \text{True}$, or either $\mathscr{I}(L_1) = \text{False}$ or $\mathscr{I}(L_2) = \text{False}$.

A sentence $\varphi$ of $W$ is semantically entailed by a set of sentences $\Gamma$ of $W$, written $\Gamma \vDash \varphi$, iff there is no interpretation $\mathscr{I}$ such that $\mathscr{I}(\psi) = \text{True}$ for every $\psi \in \Gamma$ and $\mathscr{I}(\varphi) = \text{False}$. All further semantic notions are defined similarly to classical propositional logic.

### Procedure:

Let $\Gamma_0 \subseteq \Gamma_1 \subseteq \dots \subseteq \Gamma^*$ be a finite monotonically increasing sequence of sets of sentences of $W$, where $\Gamma_0$ is the set of initial assumptions and $\Gamma^*$ is the final set.

Consider an arbitrary set $\Gamma_n$ in the sequence. Let $\Delta_n$ be any subset of $\Gamma_n$ of cardinality at most three. The procedure proceeds as follows: consider every $\Delta_n$ that has not been evaluated in any earlier step; determine all sentences $\varphi$ of $W$ semantically entailed by $\Delta_n$; and add each such $\varphi$ to $\Omega_n$. After every such $\Delta_n$ has been considered, let $\Gamma_{n+1} = \Gamma_n \cup \Omega_n$, and proceed to the next set in the sequence. This continues until every subset of cardinality at most three has been evaluated in the sequence, or every sentence of $W$ has been derived, whichever occurs first.

**Question 1. Is the procedure complete? That is, is the following true: if $\Gamma_0 \vDash \varphi$, where $\varphi$ is a sentence of $W$, then $\varphi \in \Gamma^*$?**

I argue for completeness as follows. First, every semantic consequence of $\Gamma_0$ can be obtained through a finite sequence of steps in which each step uses at most three propositions $\psi_1, \psi_2, \psi_3$ to derive a set of propositions {$\chi_1, \chi_2, \dots$} such that {$\psi_1, \psi_2, \psi_3$} $\vDash \chi_i$ for each $\chi_i$. Second, for any sentences $\psi_1, \psi_2, \psi_3$ of $W$, and any interpretation $\mathscr{I}$ such that $\mathscr{I}(\psi_1) = \text{True}$, $\mathscr{I}(\psi_2) = \text{True}$, and $\mathscr{I}(\psi_3) = \text{True}$, nothing that follows from taking $\psi_1, \psi_2, \psi_3$ together goes outside what can be written in $W$.

**Lemma 1.** Every semantic consequence of $\Gamma_0$ can be obtained through a finite sequence of steps in which each step uses at most three propositions $\psi_1, \psi_2, \psi_3$ to derive a set of propositions {$\chi_1, \chi_2, \dots$} such that {$\psi_1, \psi_2, \psi_3$} $\vDash \chi_i$ for each $\chi_i$.

If $\Gamma_0 \vDash \varphi$, then by the completeness of propositional logic there is a finite proof of $\varphi$ from $\Gamma_0$; that is, $\Gamma_0 \vdash \varphi$. Any such proof can be arranged into a sequence of steps in which each step consists of at most three propositions $\psi_1, \psi_2, \psi_3$ and a set of propositions {$\chi_1, \chi_2, \dots$} such that {$\psi_1, \psi_2, \psi_3$} $\vdash \chi_i$ for each $\chi_i$. Since propositional logic is also sound, each such step satisfies {$\psi_1, \psi_2, \psi_3$} $\vDash \chi_i$ for each $\chi_i$. Thus, every semantic consequence of $\Gamma_0$ can be obtained through a finite sequence of steps in which each step uses at most three propositions $\psi_1, \psi_2, \psi_3$ to derive a set of propositions {$\chi_1, \chi_2, \dots$} where {$\psi_1, \psi_2, \psi_3$} $\vDash \chi_i$ for each $\chi_i$.

**Lemma 2**. For any sentences $\psi_1, \psi_2, \psi_3$ of $W$, and any interpretation $\mathscr{I}$ such that $\mathscr{I}(\psi_1) = \text{True}$, $\mathscr{I}(\psi_2) = \text{True}$, and $\mathscr{I}(\psi_3) = \text{True}$, nothing that follows from taking $\psi_1, \psi_2, \psi_3$ together goes outside what can be written in $W$.

Let $\Sigma$ be the set of all sentences of $W$ and let $\Delta$ be an arbitrary subset of $\Sigma$ of cardinality at most three. Any sentence in $\Delta$ affects interpretations in exactly one of three ways: it either is always False, forces two literals to be True, or excludes assignments in which two literals are True while two others are False. All possible interpretations of $\Delta$ are given by a combination of the following: it may be always False, may fix at most six literals to be True, and may exclude assignments in which two literals are True while two others are False, with at most three of such exclusions. Observe that for every such combination, what follows from taking them together is completely expressed by some set of sentences of $W$ containing $\bot$, $(L_1 \land L_2)$, or $(L_1 \land L_2) \to (L_3 \land L_4)$. Hence, for any sentences $\psi_1, \psi_2, \psi_3$ of $W$, and any interpretation $\mathscr{I}$ such that $\mathscr{I}(\psi_1) = \text{True}$, $\mathscr{I}(\psi_2) = \text{True}$, and $\mathscr{I}(\psi_3) = \text{True}$, nothing that follows from taking $\psi_1, \psi_2, \psi_3$ together goes outside what can be written in $W$.

Therefore, by Lemma 1, every semantic consequence of $\Gamma_0$ can be written as a finite sequence of steps in which each step takes at most three sentences $\psi_1, \psi_2, \psi_3$ of $W$ and yields a set of sentences ${\chi_1, \chi_2, \dots}$ of $W$ such that {$\psi_1, \psi_2, \psi_3$} $\vDash \chi_i$ for each $\chi_i$. By Lemma 2, everything that arises at each step is completely expressible by sentences of $W$. Since the procedure considers every subset of at most three sentences in the sequence, all such steps are reproduced and every consequence is eventually generated. Hence, if $\Gamma_0 \vDash \varphi$ and $\varphi$ is a sentence of $W$, then $\varphi \in \Gamma^*$.

Alternatively, I provide a second argument. For every $\varphi$, where $\varphi$ is a sentence of $W$, if $\Gamma_0 \vDash \varphi$, then there exists a natural deduction derivation of $\varphi$ from $\Gamma_0$, and each such derivation can be simulated entirely within $W$. Considering Lemma 1 in light of this, it follows that $\varphi \in \Gamma^*$.

1. *Conjunction Introduction*. { $(L_1 \wedge L_2), (L_3 \wedge L_4)$ } $\vDash$ { $(L_1 \wedge L_3), (L_1 \wedge L_4), (L_2 \wedge L_3), (L_2 \wedge L_4)$ }.

2. *Conjunction Elimination*. { $(L_1 \wedge L_2)$ } $\vDash$ { $(L_1 \wedge L_1)$ } and { $(L_1 \wedge L_2)$ } $\vDash$ { $(L_2 \wedge L_2)$ }.

3. *Modus Ponens*. { $(L_1 \wedge L_2) \to (L_3 \wedge L_4), (L_1 \wedge L_2)$ } $\vDash$ { $(L_3 \wedge L_4)$ }.

4. *Modus Tollens*. { $(L_1 \wedge L_2) \to (L_3 \wedge L_4), (L_3^c \wedge L_4^c)$ } $\vDash$ { $(L_1^c \wedge L_2^c)$ }.

5. *Hypothetical Syllogism*. { $(L_1 \wedge L_2) \to (L_3 \wedge L_4), (L_3 \wedge L_4) \to (L_5 \wedge L_6)$ } $\vDash$ { $(L_1 \wedge L_2) \to (L_5 \wedge L_6)$ }.

6. *Disjunction Introduction*. { $(L_1 \wedge L_2)$ } $\vDash$ { $(L_1^c \wedge L_2^c) \to (L_3 \wedge L_4)$ }.

7. *Disjunction Elimination*. Since disjunctions are represented by implication, disjunction elimination reduces to applications of Modus Ponens or Modus Tollens.

8. *Or Elimination*. { $(L_1^c \wedge L_1^c) \to (L_2 \wedge L_2), (L_1 \wedge L_1) \to (L_3 \wedge L_4), (L_2 \wedge L_2) \to (L_3 \wedge L_4)$ } $\vDash$ { $(L_3 \wedge L_4)$ }.

9. *Bottom Elimination*. { $(L_1 \wedge L_2) \to (L_3 \wedge L_4), (L_1 \wedge L_2) \to (L_3^c \wedge L_4^c)$ } $\vDash$ { $(L_1 \wedge L_1) \to (L_2^c \wedge L_2^c), (L_2 \wedge L_2) \to (L_1^c \wedge L_1^c)$ } or { $(L_1^c \wedge L_2^c)$ }.

10. *Negation Elimination*. { $(L_1 \wedge L_2), (L_1^c \wedge L_2^c)$ } $\vDash$ { $\bot$ }.

Since the procedure guarantees to produce the semantic consequences of any set of sentences of $W$ that has at most three sentences in the sequence, if there is no interpretation where $(L_1 \wedge L_2)$ is true and $(L_3 \wedge L_4)$ is false, then $\vDash (L_1 \wedge L_2) \to (L_3 \wedge L_4)$. Hence any subproof can be simulated. By Lemma 1, every semantic consequence of $\Gamma_0$ can be obtained by a finite sequence of steps in which each step uses at most three sentences and, as enumerated above, each corresponding inference rules uses at most three sentences. Therefore if $\Gamma_0 \vDash \varphi$ and $\varphi$ is a sentence of $W$, then $\varphi \in \Gamma^*$.

If the statement "if $\Gamma_0 \vDash \varphi$, where $\varphi$ is a sentence of $W$, then $\varphi \in \Gamma^*$" is not true, may you provide a counterexample? Please provide an explicit $\Gamma_0$ and an explicit sentence $\varphi$ of $W$ such that $\Gamma_0 \vDash \varphi$ and $\varphi \notin \Gamma^*$. **Question 2. Does the procedure run in polynomial time?**

Each set of sentences $\Delta$ contains at most three sentences, and together with any candidate conclusion $\varphi$, there are at most sixteen variables in total. Hence at most $2^{16}$ assignments are required to evaluate whether $\Delta \vDash \varphi$, which is constant time. The number of distinct sentences in $W$ is $\Theta(n^4)$, where $n$ is the number of variables, since each sentence involves at most four variables. Thus the number of such subsets $\Delta$ is $\Theta(n^{12})$. By construction, no $\Delta$ is evaluated more than once. In the naive implementation, for each $\Delta$ one checks every $\varphi$ of $W$, giving $\Theta(n^4)$ checks per $\Delta$. Therefore the total runtime is $\Theta(n^{12}) \cdot \Theta(n^4) = \Theta(n^{16})$.

Is the above calculation correct? If not, please provide a correction.
